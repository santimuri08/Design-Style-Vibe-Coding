AI Collaboration Story

Building authentic Humanist Modernism through human-AI partnership

A reflection on how strategic direction and AI capability combined to create reader-centered design

The Partnership Model

This project demonstrates that effective AI collaboration isn't about AI "doing the work"—it's about combining human design judgment with AI's technical execution capabilities. I directed, iterated, and evaluated. Claude coded, optimized, and refined. Neither alone could have produced this result.

Going into this project, I had a critical realization: I couldn't effectively use AI without deeply understanding Humanist Modernism first. So I spent my first two hours not touching code—just researching principles, studying references, and understanding the philosophy.

That investment paid off. When I finally started collaborating with Claude, I could give precise direction: "19px body text with 1.75 line-height, not the standard 16px/1.5" instead of vague requests like "make it more readable."

Phase 1: Research & Strategic Foundation

Deep Learning Before Building (2 hours)

My Approach: I treated the style guide like a textbook. Read it three times, took notes, highlighted key principles. The goal wasn't just to know Humanist Modernism's characteristics—it was to understand the underlying philosophy: design serves human readers, not abstract aesthetic ideals.

Key Research Questions I Asked Claude:

  • "Why are humanist typefaces more readable than geometric ones?"
  • "What's the scientific backing for 60-75 characters per line?"
  • "How do warm colors affect reading comfort?"

Claude's Role: Answered technical questions, explained typography history, helped me understand readability research. But Claude couldn't tell me what Humanist Modernism feels like—I had to develop that intuition through studying references like iA Writer and Medium.com.

Critical Insight: AI is excellent at explaining principles but can't substitute for building your own design taste. I had to develop the ability to look at a website and judge: "Is this authentically humanist, or just modern design painted warm?"

Phase 2: Foundation Building - Typography System

The 16px Mistake (Iteration 1)

What Happened: I initially asked Claude to "set up the basic typography." Claude defaulted to web standards: 16px body text, 1.5 line-height. Technically correct, but not humanist.

How I Caught It: I actually read three paragraphs of the content. Felt cramped. Eyes scanning quickly without comfort. Compared to iA Writer side-by-side—theirs felt spacious, mine felt dense.

The Problem: I hadn't been specific enough. "Basic typography" to an AI means industry defaults, not style-authentic implementation.

Getting Specific (Iteration 2)

My Revised Direction:

"Set body text to 19px minimum, not 16px. Line-height must be 1.75 for comfortable reading—research shows this reduces eye strain. Use Open Sans (humanist sans) not system fonts. Max content width 720px for optimal 60-75 character line length."

Claude's Response: Generated clean CSS with proper CSS custom properties, implemented the exact specifications. Much better!

Testing: Read the same three paragraphs. Immediately more comfortable. Measured character count per line: 68 characters—right in the optimal range.

Lesson Learned: Precision matters. "Bigger text" is vague. "19px with 1.75 line-height" gets results. AI responds to measurements, not feelings.

Color Palette Challenge (Iteration 3)

The Problem: Initial version used #000 on #FFF (pure black on pure white). Technically high contrast (21:1 ratio), but felt harsh and digital—not warm and human.

My Research: Compared three options using WebAIM Contrast Checker:

  • #000 on #FFF: 21:1 ratio → Too harsh
  • #444 on #F5F5F5: 9.7:1 ratio → Better but still cold
  • #333 on #FAFAFA: 12.6:1 ratio → Warm AND accessible (exceeds WCAG AAA)

My Direction to Claude: "Replace all text colors with #333333 on #FAFAFA backgrounds. Verify this meets WCAG AAA (7:1 minimum). The goal is warmth while maintaining accessibility."

Result: Site immediately felt warmer and more inviting while remaining perfectly accessible.

Key Insight: Claude can calculate contrast ratios perfectly, but I had to make the aesthetic judgment about what "feels warm." Human intuition + AI precision = optimal result.

Phase 3: Component Design - Navigation & Cards

Navigation Iterations: Finding the Sweet Spot

Round 1 - Too Geometric:

First navigation had sharp corners (border-radius: 0) and high-contrast hover states. Looked at it and immediately thought: "This feels brutalist, not humanist."

Round 2 - Too Soft:

Overcorrected with heavy rounded corners (border-radius: 12px). Now it looked childish and unprofessional.

Round 3 - Just Right:

Refined to subtle 4px border-radius, soft hover background (#E8DCC4), and visible 2px focus outlines. Tested with keyboard navigation (Tab key)—every element clearly indicated focus state.

What This Taught Me: AI can generate code quickly, but finding the right aesthetic balance requires human iteration and testing. I went through three versions before getting the warmth-to-professionalism ratio correct.

Principle Cards: Warmth Through Details

Design Challenge: Cards needed to feel warm and inviting, not corporate and cold. But how do you explain "warmth" to an AI?

My Approach: Instead of saying "make it warmer," I described specific technical attributes:

  • "Use soft box-shadow (0 2px 8px rgba(0,0,0,0.1)) not harsh drop-shadows"
  • "Add 4px left border in warm taupe (#D4A574)"
  • "Generous padding (48px) creates breathing room"
  • "Subtle hover lift (translateY(-2px)) feels responsive, not aggressive"

Claude's Execution: Translated these specifications into clean, maintainable CSS with proper transitions and states.

Testing Process: Hovered over cards, checked hover states, verified the subtle shadow increase. Looked at the page as a whole—did it feel warm? Yes. Mission accomplished.

Phase 4: Responsive Design & Accessibility

Mobile Testing Revealed Critical Issues

The Wake-Up Call: Desktop version looked perfect. Opened it on my phone—disaster. Navigation links overlapped, touch targets were too small (38×36px, below the 44×44px minimum), hero text felt overwhelming at 48px on a small screen.

Why This Happened: I'd been designing and testing primarily in Chrome DevTools. The device simulator is helpful, but nothing replaces testing on an actual phone with actual fingers.

Solutions Implemented:

  1. Navigation: Changed to vertical stack at 768px breakpoint
  2. Touch Targets: Increased padding to ensure minimum 44×44px on all interactive elements
  3. Typography: Reduced hero from 48px to 36px on mobile (still prominent but not overwhelming)
  4. Spacing: Adjusted margins and padding specifically for mobile comfort

Major Learning: AI can generate responsive CSS, but only human testing on real devices can catch usability issues. I tested on iPhone SE (375px), iPad (768px), and desktop (1920px) before considering it "done."

Accessibility: Beyond Code Compliance

Initial Lighthouse Accessibility Score: 93/100. Not bad, but not excellent.

Issues Claude Identified:

  • Missing alt text on images
  • Heading hierarchy skipped levels (h1 to h3)
  • No skip link for keyboard users
  • Some focus states insufficiently visible

But I Did My Own Testing Too:

  • Keyboard Navigation: Tabbed through entire site—verified every interactive element reachable and clearly indicated
  • Screen Reader: Tested with NVDA on Windows—all content properly announced, navigation landmarks clear
  • Color Blindness: Used Chrome's vision deficiency simulator—content remained readable in all modes

Final Score After Fixes: 100/100 on Accessibility. But more importantly, the site was genuinely usable by people with diverse abilities.

Reflection: Claude is excellent at identifying technical accessibility issues (missing alt text, ARIA labels). But human testing with actual assistive technologies catches real-world usability problems that automated tools miss.

Phase 5: Polish & Quality Assurance

Lighthouse Optimization

First Run (Before Optimization):

  • Performance: 87 (below target)
  • Accessibility: 93 (good but improvable)
  • Best Practices: 92 (minor issues)
  • SEO: 100 (perfect)

My Direction to Claude: "Audit shows Performance at 87. Primary issue is font loading. Add preconnect for Google Fonts. Also fix the accessibility issues you flagged."

Claude's Implementation: 

<link rel="preconnect" href="https://fonts.googleapis.com">
<link rel="preconnect" href="https://fonts.gstatic.com" crossorigin>

Second Run (After Fixes):

  • Performance: 95 ✓
  • Accessibility: 100 ✓
  • Best Practices: 95 ✓
  • SEO: 100 ✓

All scores above 90. Ready for submission.

What Made This Collaboration Effective

1. Deep Knowledge Came First

I spent 2 hours researching before writing any code. This wasn't wasted time—it was the foundation that made all subsequent collaboration effective. When I understood why Humanist Modernism uses 19px text and warm colors, I could direct Claude with confidence.

AI can't substitute for your design understanding. It can execute your vision, but you need to develop that vision first.

2. Precision in Communication

What Didn't Work: "Make it look more humanist" (too vague)

What Worked: "Set body text to 19px with 1.75 line-height, use #333 on #FAFAFA, max-width 720px" (specific and measurable)

AI responds to measurements and specifications, not feelings and aesthetics. Translate your design intuition into technical requirements.

3. Ruthless Iteration

I never accepted the first version of anything. Typography went through 3 iterations. Navigation went through 3 iterations. Colors went through 3 iterations. Each round got closer to authentic Humanist Modernism.

The willingness to say "this is good but not excellent—let's refine it" is what separates adequate projects from exceptional ones.

4. Human Testing Was Essential

Claude could calculate contrast ratios (12.6:1) and generate responsive CSS. But Claude couldn't:

  • Feel whether 19px text was comfortable to read for 5 minutes
  • Judge if the color palette felt "warm" or "cold"
  • Test whether touch targets were actually easy to tap
  • Evaluate if the design felt authentically humanist

AI augments human judgment, it doesn't replace it. Physical testing and aesthetic evaluation remain human responsibilities.

5. Visual References Bridged Communication Gaps

When words failed, screenshots worked. Showing Claude "this is what iA Writer does" communicated more effectively than describing it. Comparing my site to reference images helped identify specific issues.

AI processes visual information differently than humans, but screenshots provide a shared reference point for iteration.

Challenges & How We Overcame Them

Challenge 1: Defining "Warmth" Technically

Problem: I could look at a design and feel it was "cold" or "warm," but how do you explain that to an AI?

Solution: Broke "warmth" down into measurable components:

  • Color: #333 instead of #000 (less harsh)
  • Spacing: 1.75 line-height instead of 1.5 (more breathing room)
  • Shadows: Soft (0.1 opacity) instead of hard
  • Corners: 4-6px border-radius instead of 0 or 12px
  • Borders: Accent colors instead of pure black

Learning: Abstract design concepts need translation into specific technical specifications before AI can implement them.

Challenge 2: Mobile Testing Lag

Problem: Developed on desktop, didn't test mobile until late. Discovered major usability issues.

Solution: Adopted a test-as-you-build approach. After each component, checked on actual phone. Caught issues early when they were easier to fix.

Learning: DevTools device simulator is convenient but incomplete. Real device testing is non-negotiable.

Challenge 3: Balancing Density and Whitespace

Problem: First version had too much whitespace (felt empty). Second version had too little (felt cramped).

Solution: Printed pages to see actual density. Asked a non-designer friend to read content and give feedback. Adjusted spacing iteratively based on real reading experience, not just visual appearance.

Learning: Sometimes you need to step away from the screen and evaluate design in different contexts (print, different devices, different lighting).

Key Insights About AI Collaboration

AI's Strengths:

  • Rapid code generation with clean structure
  • Technical implementation of specific requirements
  • Calculating measurements and ratios accurately
  • Identifying accessibility issues automatically
  • Optimizing performance systematically
  • Maintaining consistency across multiple pages

Human's Essential Role:

  • Aesthetic judgment ("Does this feel warm?")
  • User experience testing (actual reading/navigation)
  • Design philosophy understanding
  • Iterative refinement decisions
  • Physical device testing
  • Evaluating authenticity to design style

The Sweet Spot: AI handles technical execution while human maintains design vision and quality standards. Neither is effective alone—the partnership creates results neither could achieve independently.

Would I Recommend This Approach?

Absolutely—but with important caveats.

This Works If You:

  • Invest in deep learning first — Can't direct AI without understanding principles
  • Communicate with precision — Vague requests get vague results
  • Iterate relentlessly — First drafts are starting points, not endpoints
  • Test physically — On real devices with real interactions
  • Maintain design judgment — Don't defer aesthetic decisions to AI
  • Document thoroughly — Track iterations and decisions

This Doesn't Work If You:

  • Skip the research phase and jump straight to building
  • Accept AI's first output without iteration
  • Give vague directions like "make it better"
  • Never test on actual devices
  • Let AI make all design decisions
  • Don't develop your own aesthetic judgment

Final Reflection

This project taught me that AI collaboration is fundamentally about effective partnership, not automation.

I came in thinking AI would "speed up the coding." What I learned is that AI's real value is enabling rapid iteration—I could test three different typography scales in an hour, compare them, and choose the best. That iteration speed let me achieve a level of refinement that would have taken much longer manually.

But speed without direction is just motion. The critical skill was developing design judgment—the ability to look at implementation and evaluate: "Is this authentically Humanist Modernism? Does it serve the reader? Does it feel warm and accessible?"

AI provided the technical capability. I provided the vision, taste, and quality standards. Together, we created something that prioritizes what Humanist Modernism is truly about: design that serves human readers first.
